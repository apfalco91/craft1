What Award Shows' Winners Correlate with the Oscars?
by Anthony Falco

Screen Actors Guild Awards

The Screen Actors Guild Awards, which has only been around since 1995, is the award show for the American labor union that represents performers in television and film. Many claim it is an indicator for the Oscar's four major acting categories – Best Actor, Actress, Supporting Actor and Supporting Actress. The nominees are chosen by 2,100 members that are randomly selected. The winners are then picked by the 100,000-plus population of the organization, according to SAG.

Golden Globes

The award show that is synonymous with comedian Ricky Gervais, the Golden Globes was started in 1944 by the Hollywood Foreign Press Association. Currently, they have a format that includes two Best Picture, Actor and Actress winners – each for drama and comedy/musical. The winners are chosen by "about 90" members of the press organization, according to the HFPA.

Return

Directors Guild of America Awards

Started in 1948, the Directors Guild of America Awards commemorates the very best directors of film, television, documentaries, commericials and news. However, since it is an award show by and for the director's union, out of this study's six major categories, they only choose Best Director. DGA has yet to respond to questions about their voting procedures.

Return

Producers Guild of America Awards

The Producers Guild of America Awards was born in 1990 to honor the producers of television, film and news. The American labor union represents over 7,000 members of the three mediums and meets every year to honor a Best Picture. Like the DGA, the PGA only gives out one of the six major awards.

Return

National Board of Review Awards

Largely considered one of the first award shows, since 1930 its membership has grown to include academics, critics, filmmakers and more. Each of whom have the opportunity to vote for the National Board of Review's esteem prizes, which includes Best Picture, Actor, Actress, Supporting Actor, Supporting Actress and Director.

Return

American Cinema Editors Eddie Awards

The American Cinema editors were originally formed by Warren Low and Jack Ogilvie in 1950, according to ACE. The award show, in its earliest iteration, took place the following year. However, in 1962 the group branched out to form the Eddie awards, which is strictly voted on by the industry's editors. Like PGA or DGA, this ceremony only has one award that is similar to the Oscars – Best Edited Movie – which is their representation for Best Picture.

Return

British Academy of Film and Television Arts

The British Academy of Film and Television Arts is a charity that seeks to expand upon the arts in the two aforementioned mediums – and has even branched out into video games. The voting process is done by their 7,500 members who then submit their choices in their respective fields.

Return

Independent Spirit Awards

Since 1984, the Independent Spirit Awards honors the indies of the year. Though the definition of an indie has evolved over the last decade, it mainly means a movie that was entirely or majority funded outside Hollywood's studio system. As for the ceremony, the winners are chosen by members of Film Independent and Independent Filmmaker Project organizations, which includes both industry members as well as critics and academics.

Return

Critics' Choice Awards

The Critics' Choice Awards was first started in 1996, and it picks the very best in the movie industry. The current voting board has 308 critics, who are from the print, radio, Internet and broadcast mediums – all of whom are listed on the Critics' Choice's website.

Return

Satellite Awards

Originally started in 1996, the Satellite Awards winners are chosen by the International Press Academy. The IPA is made up of international and domestic journalists in the print, blogger, broadcast and radio mediums, according to the organization's website.

Return

New York Film Critics Circle Awards

The New York Film Critics Circle was originally formed in 1935. Made up of the city's 32 critics from various fields, they meet every December to vote on the very best of the previous year. They call their winners "a principled alternative to the Oscars, honoring esthetic merit in a forum that is immune to commercial and political pressures."

Return

Los Angeles Film Critic Association Awards

Similiar to the New York Film Critics Circle, the Los Angeles Film Critic Association serves as an alternative to the Oscars. Founded in 1975, it is currently made up of 54 critics that appear on a variety of formats in the Los Angeles area. They meet every December to vote on the best of the previous year.

Return

National Society of Film Critics Awards

The National Society of Film Critics was founded in 1966. Every January, 55 members meet to award who they think is the best from the previous year. The voting happens in a closed session and the results are shipped out to studios and the press immediately after, according to the organization's procedures.

Is it possible to predict Oscar winners? This question has plagued journalists, filmmakers, statisticians and more. But there are so many variables to worry about. Who could have honestly guessed that Crash would defeat Brokeback Mountain or Shakespeare in Love would overtake Saving Private Ryan?

While accounting for these variables may be impossible or reserved for more talented statisticians, there is a way, at the least, to better understand these trends. Simply, let's look at the winners of the award shows prior to the Oscars: how many times have they correlated with the Academy Awards over the last 15 years?

Now, each picture displays one of the thirteen shows or critic choice awards that happen prior to the Oscars – the only one that is left out is the Gotham Awards, which was removed due to its smaller sample size. Each logo is clickable, leading to an explainer about the awards as well as showing the average, per year and per category, times each have chosen the same winner as the Academy.

First and foremost, lets look at the award with the best overall average. Though the Directors Guild of America (DGA) has only one award that qualifies – Best Director – it has had the same winner as the Oscars in 12 of the last 15 years. In fact, if one were to go beyond the time parameters, then the DGA have had different winners only seven times, which is an astounding feat. If one looked at the Producers Guild of America (PGA), they have missed seven times in just the last 25 years.

Back to the parameters, the PGA ties the Critic's Choice as they both pick the Oscars' Best Picture winner 73 percent of the time. Statistically, picking this landmark reward is the hardest, as it has the overall lowest average among all the categories: it sits at 40 percent, with the highest being best actor with 49 percent.

As for the acting categories, one would expect the Screen Actors Guild – which is the go to example when trying to predict the Academy Awards' acting winners – to have the highest percentage in those categories. While it still remains near the top of all four, it never out right wins one of them. The SAG ties the Golden Globes and Critics' Choice for Best Actor. It comes in second to the Golden Globes with both actress and supporting actor. And ties British Academy for Film and Television Arts for supporting actress. Peoples' use of SAG when it comes to an Oscar predictor might be due to the last three years, where they have never gotten more than one category wrong.

Finally, the shows with the overall worst percentage: the worst by far is the National Board of Review, which only got the same winner as the Oscars 19 percent of the time. Not very far behind are the National Society of Film Critics and the Independent Spirit Awards with 23 and 25 percent respectively. And it appears both Los Angeles and New York Critics' picks truly are an alternative to the Oscars, as they round out the bottom five.

In the end, even looking at these indicators, this does not provide the whole picture: meaning it is impossible to just use this information to guess this year's Oscar winners. However, if one were to take the highest percentage of each category, then the Best Picture would be either The Big Short or Spotlight – the latter is starting to gain steam.

The two acting would go to Leonardo DiCaprio and Brie Larson. The supporting actress is a toss up between Alicia Vikander and Kate Winslett, but actor will likely go to Stallone. Finally, Best Director is going to have a repeat winner with Alejandro González Iñárritu. But we can only wait and see if these predictions have any merit.
